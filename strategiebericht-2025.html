Executive Summary: The Convergence of Stagnation and the Imperative for Renewal

The Alpine Rhine Valley economic region‚Äîa geographically compact but industrially dense cluster spanning the Austrian state of Vorarlberg, the eastern Swiss cantons (especially St. Gallen and Thurgau), and the Principality of Liechtenstein‚Äîis at a historic turning point. As someone who has held operational and strategic responsibility in three of the most formative industrial companies in this region‚Äîin automation technology at Heron, in automotive connectivity at Hirschmann, and in control electronics at Bachmann‚ÄîI observe a dangerous divergence between the technological excellence of the hardware and the obsolescence of the underlying digital processes.

In 2025, we find ourselves in a phase of "stagformation." This term describes the convergence of economic stagnation, driven by geopolitical uncertainties and weakness in core sales markets, with a brutal transformation imperative triggered by technological disruptions (AI, e-mobility, cloud computing). The data is clear: by the end of 2024, Vorarlberg had seen no substantial growth for over 14 quarters.1 The eastern Swiss export industry is struggling with a strong franc and declining order backlogs from the Eurozone.2 Liechtenstein, often a rock of stability, is feeling the cooling of global demand for capital goods directly in its export statistics.3

This report is not a theoretical macro paper. It is an operational analysis from the engine room of the industry. It explains why the region is at risk of losing its competitiveness, not because it builds bad products, but because it develops and manufactures these products with outdated IT systems, isolated data silos, and an obsolete understanding of value creation. The thesis of this report is: to secure the technological sovereignty of the Alpine Rhine Valley, we must radically shift our focus from pure hardware optimization to mastering the digital value chain. This requires a painful departure from legacy ERP systems, the introduction of genuine CI/CD pipelines in embedded development, and the courage for business-model-changing servitization.

1. Macroeconomic and Regional Situation Analysis: Navigating Headwinds

The Alpine Rhine Valley region has established itself over decades as the "Silicon Valley of Mechatronics." The density of world market leaders in niche areas‚Äîfrom cable car technology and Blum fittings to highly specialized automotive connectors‚Äîis almost unique worldwide. But this success model, based on exporting high-precision hardware from a high-wage country, is eroding under the changed conditions of 2024 and 2025.

1.1. Economic Stagnation and Structural Weakness

Current economic data paints a picture of a region that operates excellently on an operational level but is strategically squeezed. The industry, traditionally the engine of growth, is sputtering.

Vorarlberg: The End of Automatic Growth
The Federation of Vorarlberg Industry provides alarming figures. In the third quarter of 2025, the business climate index showed no sign of a positive turnaround. Particularly worrying is the companies' outlook: 89% do not expect an improvement in their earnings situation in the next six months.4 This is not a temporary dip but a structural hardening. Over a period of more than three years‚Äîeffectively since the end of the post-pandemic recovery‚Äîindustrial production has been stagnating or declining.

The reasons are complex, but one factor stands out: the cost explosion at the location. With a tax rate of around 44% in Austria and massively increased unit labor costs, the air is getting thin for the production of standard goods.1 When Vorarlberg companies export products, they increasingly also export the high local costs, which becomes more and more difficult in global competition against Asia and even the USA (with its lower energy costs and subsidies).

Eastern Switzerland: Currency Pressure and Export Dependency
The situation in Eastern Switzerland is similar, though accentuated by currency effects. The cantons of St. Gallen and Thurgau are traditionally heavily focused on the mechanical, electrical, and metal (MEM) sector. The economic report of the IHK St. Gallen-Appenzell for November 2025 speaks of "rough headwinds."2 While the domestic sector (construction, services) and consumption still have a stabilizing effect, foreign demand is collapsing.

A specific problem for Eastern Switzerland is its exposure to the German market. As Germany itself struggles with de-industrialization and weakness in the automotive sector, this has a direct impact on the Rhine Valley via the supply chain. The hope that the USA will step in as a growth engine is dampened by protectionist trade policies and tariffs, even though these have been partially reduced.2 Companies report "above-average uncertainty," which is poison for investment decisions.

Liechtenstein: Stability with Cracks
The Principality of Liechtenstein often proves more resilient, thanks to a strong financial sector and highly specialized industrial champions (Hilti, Ivoclar, Hilcona). But here, too, the data for 2025 shows cracks in the facade. Direct goods exports fell by 3.7% in the first quarter of 2025.3 Although the general situation is still assessed by companies as "satisfactory," the trend is downward.

1.2. The Geopolitical Dimension and Locational Competition

From my experience at Heron and Hirschmann, I know that decisions about new production lines today are rarely made in favor of the Rhine Valley when it comes to pure capacity expansion. Plants in Morocco (like the new Hirschmann plant in Oujda 5) or Eastern Europe are no longer just "extended workbenches" but state-of-the-art manufacturing facilities.

The Alpine Rhine Valley region no longer competes on the price of a labor hour, but on the productivity of a labor hour. And this is precisely the problem: productivity gains in recent years have lagged behind cost increases. We have optimized processes (Lean Management, Kaizen), but not revolutionized them. An SMT line in Dornbirn does not physically run faster than one in Shenzhen. The difference must lie in how this line is controlled, maintained, and integrated into the supply chain.

Another aspect is bureaucracy. The Federation of Vorarlberg Industry cites projects delayed by administrative hurdles and objections as symptomatic of the location.1 If building a heat pump or expanding a hall takes years, the location loses its most important characteristic: agility. In comparison, the Hirschmann plant in Morocco was built in just ten months.5 This difference in speed is an existential threat.

1.3. The Skills Shortage as an Innovation Brake

Paradoxically, we suffer from stagnation and a shortage of skilled workers at the same time. The "war for talent" is real. In the electronics industry in Germany alone, there is a shortage of 100,000 engineers, a gap that is proportionally reflected in our region.6 In Vorarlberg and Eastern Switzerland, we compete for the same pool of skilled workers.

The problem is exacerbated by the fact that young, well-educated talents (Gen Z) have different expectations of their work environment. They do not want to work with AS/400 screens or outdated SAP GUIs. They expect cloud-native tools, home office options, and agile working methods. The technological backwardness of many traditional companies (legacy IT) thus becomes an obstacle in recruiting. Anyone still using Excel lists for error management today loses the best minds to tech companies or start-ups.

2. The Industry Crisis: Automotive and Electronics in Upheaval

As a former manager at Hirschmann Automotive (Tier-1/Tier-2 supplier) and Bachmann electronic (automation/energy), I know the specific pains of these key industries. We are experiencing a fundamental shift in power relations and technological requirements.

2.1. The Automotive Shock: Stagformation and Chinese Dominance

For decades, the automotive industry was the pacemaker for the industry in Vorarlberg and Eastern Switzerland. But the engine is sputtering. Studies by Strategy& and Roland Berger show that global vehicle production is stagnating and will not reach 2019 levels in Europe even by 2030.7

The EV Dilemma
We have worked for years towards the ramp-up of electric mobility (BEV). Plants were converted, high-voltage expertise was built up (as at Hirschmann with the HPS40 connectors 9). But now we are seeing a cooling of BEV demand in Europe and the USA, driven by the phasing out of subsidies and a lack of charging infrastructure. At the same time, Chinese OEMs (BYD, Nio, Xiaomi) are entering the market with technically superior and cheaper vehicles.

For a supplier in the Rhine Valley, this means:

  • Volume Uncertainty: Planned call-offs for European EV platforms are being revised downwards.
  • Price Pressure: To compete with Chinese rivals, OEMs are demanding massive price cuts. Suppliers' margins are already at a historic low (EBIT margins below 5% 10).
  • Technology Gap: Chinese manufacturers develop vehicles in 24 months, while European ones take 48. Suppliers must radically accelerate their development cycles to keep up.

The Commoditization of Hardware
A wiring harness or a connector is a commodity today. Differentiation is no longer just about hardware quality (which is a given), but about system competence. A supplier must not only deliver the connector but also the thermal model for it, the digital twin for the customer's simulation, and the component's CO2 footprint at the push of a button. Those who cannot do this will be sorted out.

2.2. Electronics Manufacturing (SMT): The Battle for Components and Data

During my time as Director of SMT Manufacturing at Bachmann, I experienced the fragility of supply chains firsthand. The chip crisis may have faded from the headlines, but the structural problems remain.6

  • Legacy Chips: The automotive and industrial sectors often require older chip generations (40nm and older), for which hardly any new capacities are being built. Allocation remains a constant risk.
  • Obsolescence Management: As the life cycles of consumer electronics (mobile phones) dictate chip availability, long-lasting industrial goods (wind turbine controls, industrial controls) must be constantly redesigned because components are discontinued. This ties up enormous R&D capacities that are missing for innovation.

However, the biggest challenge in SMT manufacturing is not the hardware, but data consistency. A modern SMT line (printer, SPI, pick-and-place, reflow, AOI) is a data powerhouse. But in many factories in the Rhine Valley, this data is not used to regulate the process in real time ("closed loop"), but only for passive documentation. This is a waste of capital.

3. Operational Deep Dive I: Manufacturing and IT Integration

The strategic problems manifest themselves concretely on the shop floor. This is where it is decided whether a company is efficient enough to survive at the Alpine Rhine Valley location.

3.1. The "Legacy ERP Money Pit"

Many companies in the region rely on ERP systems that were introduced 15 or 20 years ago. These are often heavily modified SAP ECC systems or older Microsoft versions (Navision/AX).

The Problem of "Over-Customization":
In my time at Heron and other positions, the ERP was often an obstacle. Over the years, individual processes were hard-coded into the ABAP code ("Z-transactions"). The result is a monolithic system that can no longer be updated ("version lock" 11).

  • Costs: Maintenance and operation of these legacy systems consume up to 90% of the IT budget. There is no money left for innovation.
  • Data Silos: The ERP does not know what is happening in production. Inventory levels do not match because material withdrawals are booked with a time lag.
  • Cloud Incapability: These systems are not cloud-ready. The integration of AI services (e.g., for sales forecasting) fails due to a lack of APIs.

The Need for a "Clean Core":
We must have the courage to replace or radically standardize these systems. The approach must be "Clean Core." The ERP base remains standard (SaaS, Public Cloud), and the specific differentiating features (e.g., the special variant configurator for Robotunits at Heron) are built as applications alongside the ERP (Side-by-Side Extensibility) and connected via middleware.12

3.2. The Gap Between ERP and Shop Floor (MES)

In cable assembly (Hirschmann) or electronics manufacturing (Bachmann), the gap between the commercial world (ERP) and the physical world (machine) is often huge.

Scenario: A crimping machine produces 10,000 contacts per hour. The quality data (crimp force monitoring) is stored locally on the machine. The ERP only knows: "Order started" and "Order finished."

  • Risk: If a batch is defective, it is often only noticed during final inspection or by the customer.
  • Solution: Use of middleware and IIoT platforms. We need a layer that translates machine data (OPC UA, MQTT) into business processes. If the crimp force drifts, the MES must block the order in the ERP‚Äîautomatically and in milliseconds.

Local partners like Innonav (for Microsoft Dynamics) or Arcon/OBT (for Abacus) offer solutions here, but often the companies themselves lack an understanding of the need for this middleware architecture.14 They rely too much on Excel and "sneaker-net" interfaces (employees running around with USB sticks).

3.3. Traceability as a Hygiene Factor

For automotive suppliers, traceability is not a luxury, but a requirement. But how is it implemented? Often as a "graveyard database": data is stored in case it is needed (recall). This is not enough. Traceability data must be used for process mining. Why does the setup process on line 3 always take 15 minutes longer than on line 1? The data is there, but it is not being analyzed. A huge productivity potential lies dormant here.

4. Operational Deep Dive II: Validation and Simulation

As a former Head of Environmental Simulation at Hirschmann, I know: the laboratory is often the bottleneck in development.

4.1. The Separation of Physical and Virtual

In the region, we afford expensive test laboratories with shaker tables, climate chambers, and salt spray systems. In parallel, we have departments for FEM simulation (Finite Element Method).

The Problem: These worlds often do not communicate with each other.

  • The simulation calculates a service life of 5000 hours.
  • The physical test shows a failure after 3000 hours.
  • The correlation of the data is done manually‚Äîor not at all.

Requirement: We need LIMS (Laboratory Information Management Systems) that are directly coupled with PLM (Product Lifecycle Management) systems. Every physical test must be used to calibrate the virtual model. The goal is to reduce the number of physical tests by 50% by improving simulation quality. This saves months of "time-to-market."

4.2. Automated Compliance (ISO 26262 / TISAX)

The automotive industry requires compliance with strict standards such as ISO 26262 (Functional Safety) and TISAX (Information Security). Creating the documentation for this manually is a massive time sink.

In modern development environments (CI/CD), this documentation is generated automatically. When a software developer checks in code, tests are run that check for compliance and record the proof in the system.16

Many companies in the Rhine Valley still work with Word documents and manual approval processes. This is inefficient and error-prone. Automating compliance processes is a direct lever for increasing engineering productivity.

5. Strategic Realignment: Digitalization as a Business Model

If we accept that we can no longer become cheaper on the hardware side, we must increase the value of our products through software and service.

5.1. Servitization: The Shift from Capex to Opex

Traditional business models in mechanical engineering (e.g., Heron, Robotunits) are based on the sale of capital goods (Capex). The customer buys, we deliver, the invoice is issued. End of the relationship (except for spare parts). The future lies in servitization.18

Example Bachmann Electronic: Through Condition Monitoring (CMS), not only the hardware is sold, but also the "health monitoring" of the wind turbine. The customer pays for the security that the system is running.20

Potential: For machine builders in the Rhine Valley, this means: we no longer sell the assembly machine, but the "guaranteed output." We monitor the machine remotely, maintain it predictively (before it fails), and bill based on usage.

Prerequisites:

  • IoT Connectivity: The machine must be able to "phone home."
  • Data Platform: We need a cloud infrastructure that can process these amounts of data.
  • Billing Engine: The ERP must be able to bill monthly service fees or pay-per-use‚Äîwhich most legacy systems cannot do (see point 3.1).

5.2. CI/CD for Embedded Systems

The products of the region are becoming increasingly "intelligent." A high-voltage contactor from Hirschmann or a controller from Bachmann contains firmware. The development of this software must no longer follow the old waterfall model.

We need to introduce CI/CD (Continuous Integration / Continuous Deployment) pipelines, as are standard in web development.21

  • Advantage: Faster release cycles. We can deliver new functions to the customer "over-the-air."
  • Quality: Automated tests with every code change (Shift-Left Testing) prevent errors from occurring in the field.
  • Resilience: If a chip is not available and we have to port to another microcontroller, automated tests help to validate the new hardware base in days instead of months.

6. The Human Factor: Leadership and Culture in the Rhine Valley

As a leader in this region, I know: technology is often the smaller problem. The culture is the stumbling block. We are proud of our engineering skills ("Vorsprung durch Technik"), but we struggle with the "failure culture" of the software world.

6.1. Farewell to Perfectionism

In the hardware world, the product must be 100% perfect at launch (you can't patch hardware). In the digital world, "Done is better than perfect" and "Fail fast" apply. These two cultures collide in companies. The task of management is to create "ambidexterity":

  • Exploit: Hardware production must continue to be precise and error-free.
  • Explore: Digital teams must be allowed to experiment, build MVPs (Minimum Viable Products), and sometimes fail without heads rolling.

6.2. New Work in the Factory

We cannot ignore the skills shortage. We must make work more attractive.

  • IT Equipment: A modern workplace (hardware, software) is a hygiene factor.
  • Remote Work: Wherever possible (design, IT, purchasing), home office must be enabled‚Äîeven across borders (which is complex in terms of tax law between AT/CH/LI, but must be solved).
  • Automation as Relief: We must communicate that robots and AI are there to take over the boring, repetitive tasks so that people can do the creative, value-adding tasks.

7. Conclusion and Roadmap: The Agenda for 2025-2030

The companies in Vorarlberg, Eastern Switzerland, and Liechtenstein have all the prerequisites to be successful in the 21st century: capital, know-how, and a strong industrial base. But they must act now to break through the "stagformation."

Here is the concrete roadmap that I recommend from my experience as a CIO and production manager:

Phase 1: Clean Up (Clean Core & Connectivity) ‚Äì The next 12 months

  • IT Inventory: Identify all legacy systems and "shadow IT" (Excel solutions). Start a project to migrate to a standard cloud solution (Clean Core strategy).
  • Shop Floor Connectivity: Every machine must be connected. Install IoT gateways. Collect data, even if you don't know what to do with it yet. Data history is worth its weight in gold.
  • Introduce Middleware: Stop point-to-point interfaces. Implement an Enterprise Service Bus or a lean Python middleware solution.

Phase 2: Optimize (Automation & Validation) ‚Äì Months 12-24

  • Automate Processes: Use RPA for administrative processes. Use shop floor data for initial control loops (e.g., automatic material reordering).
  • Introduce LIMS: Digitize the laboratory. Couple test data with development.
  • Establish CI/CD: Automate software development and compliance checks.

Phase 3: Transform (Servitization & AI) ‚Äì Months 24-36

  • Pilot Service Models: Start a pay-per-use or performance model with a pilot customer.
  • Scale AI: Use the collected data for real AI applications (Predictive Maintenance, Predictive Quality).

The path is rocky and requires investment at a time when money may not be loose. But the alternative‚Äîa slow decline into irrelevance as an interchangeable contract manufacturer‚Äîis not an option for the proud Alpine Rhine Valley. We must refine our hardware excellence with software dominance. This is the key to long-term competitiveness.
